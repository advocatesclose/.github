# ADVOCATES CLOSE CORPORATION

When I was a summer associate, someone asked me to take a first pass through a credit agreement. I knew the task was trivial at the time but found it interesting because of something I had read a few years earlier. 

THe article was about the use of drones and machines more generally by the military. The idea was that there are certain jobs for which human beings are just very poorly suited. These include things that, as the article described it, were "dull, dirty or dangerous." 

At the time, the idea that a human being could just sit down and review four hundred pages worth of credit agreements and annexes thereto was incomprehensible. It struck me as physically impossible that any human being could ever do a thing that boring for that long without screwing up somewhere. 

The point is not that credit agreements are long or that reading is hard. The point is to minimize human error. What I sell is less of a product than it is a process. It is a process for doing things in an orderly, predictable and deterministic way. 

If you save a document to Word and then save it to PDF, you did not do two different things exaclty one time. You did exactly one thing two different times. And yet, in practice, no one would ever see it that way. In practice, you did two things--that is why you have two documents. And therein lies the problem.

The problem is that, right now, there is a one-to-one correspondence between the work that you do and the result that you get. You press "save" one time so that you may save one document. You press "run" one time so that you may run one program. And so on. The whole point of this app is to break that correspondence.

There is no reason why inputs should be related to outputs in this way.
